Platform engineering tools have become essential for organizations aiming to streamline their DevOps processes and boost developer productivity. As the complexity of modern software systems grows, these tools provide the foundation for efficient, scalable, and reliable application development and deployment. But which tools should you choose for your platform engineering stack? Let's explore the top 10 platform engineering tools that are shaping the DevOps landscape in 2024.

## What is Platform Engineering and Why It Matters

Platform engineering is the practice of designing, building, and maintaining the infrastructure and tools that support software development and operations. It aims to create a seamless, self-service experience for developers, allowing them to focus on writing code rather than managing infrastructure.

Key benefits of platform engineering include:

- Increased developer productivity: By automating repetitive tasks and providing standardized workflows, developers can focus on creating value.
- Improved reliability and scalability: Well-designed platforms ensure consistent environments and easy scaling of applications.
- Enhanced security and compliance: Centralized control over infrastructure and deployments helps enforce security best practices.
- Faster time-to-market: Streamlined processes and automated pipelines accelerate the delivery of new features and updates.

Platform engineering helps bridge the gap between development and operations teams by tackling common DevOps challenges. It provides a unified approach to infrastructure management, application deployment, and monitoring—key components of efficient DevOps practices.

Internal Developer Platforms (IDPs) are often the result of successful platform engineering efforts. These platforms provide a curated set of tools and services that enable developers to self-serve infrastructure and deployment needs, further enhancing productivity and standardization across an organization. IDPs not only empower developers to handle infrastructure autonomously, but also integrate tools for monitoring, observability, and performance management, providing a holistic platform for software development.”

## Essential Components of a Platform Engineering Stack

A robust platform engineering stack typically includes tools for:

1. Infrastructure provisioning and management: Tools like Terraform, Pulumi, or AWS CloudFormation.
2. Container orchestration: Example: Kubernetes, Docker Swarm.
3. CI/CD: Jenkins, GitLab CI, and CircleCI can be used for CI/CD.
4. Monitoring and observability: Prometheus, Grafana, Datadog.
5. Developer portals and self-service interfaces: Backstage, OpenShift, or custom-built IDPs.

These components work together to create a cohesive environment where developers can easily provision resources, deploy applications, and monitor performance—all while adhering to organizational standards and best practices.

The key to a successful platform engineering stack lies in the integration and interoperability of its tools. When selecting tools, consider how well they integrate with your existing systems and with each other. This integration ensures a smooth flow of information and processes across your entire development lifecycle. Integration not only ensures smooth workflows but also reduces bottlenecks in the pipeline, allowing for faster feedback and more reliable deployments.

Balancing customization and standardization is crucial in platform engineering. While standardization promotes consistency and reduces complexity, some level of customization is often necessary to meet specific team or project requirements. For instance, while a standardized CI/CD pipeline ensures consistency across teams, a custom deployment script might be required to handle edge cases for specific applications. The goal is to find the right balance that provides flexibility without sacrificing the benefits of a standardized approach.

## Top 10 Platform Engineering Tools for 2024

### 1. Kubernetes: The Foundation of Container Orchestration

<a href="https://kubernetes.io/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Kubernetes</a> has become the de facto standard for container orchestration in platform engineering. It provides a powerful system for automating the deployment, scaling, and management of containerized applications. 

<Figure src="/img/comparisons/2024/08/platform-engineering-tools-Screenshot_2024-08-23_at_3.14.57_PM.webp" alt="Kubernetes" caption="Kubernetes" />

Key features of Kubernetes include:

- Automated rollouts and rollbacks: Easily update applications and revert to previous versions if issues arise.
- Service discovery and load balancing: Automatically distributes traffic and exposes services internally or to the internet.
- Self-healing: Restart failed containers, replace and reschedule containers when nodes die, and kill containers that don't respond to health checks.
- Secret and configuration management: Deploy and update secrets and application configuration without rebuilding your image.

Kubernetes also enables scalability by automatically managing workloads and adjusting the number of running containers based on resource utilization and demand. It allows for easy scaling up or down of applications, ensuring optimal performance and cost efficiency. Portability is achieved through its container-based architecture, which allows applications to run consistently across different environments, such as on-premises, public clouds, or hybrid setups. This flexibility makes it easier to move applications between environments without modifying them, promoting a more seamless and consistent development and deployment process.

How to get the most out of it?

- Implement a robust CI/CD pipeline for Kubernetes deployments.
- Use namespaces to organize and isolate resources within a cluster.
- Leverage Helm charts for packaging and versioning Kubernetes applications.
- Implement proper resource requests and limits to ensure efficient cluster utilization.

### 2. Docker: Streamlining Application Containerization

<a href="https://www.docker.com/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Docker</a> simplifies the process of creating, deploying, and running applications by using containers. It packages an application and its dependencies into a standardized unit for software development and deployment.

Key Docker concepts include:

- Images: Read-only templates containing an application and its dependencies.
- Containers: Runnable instances of Docker images.
- Registries: Repositories for storing and sharing Docker images.

Best practices for using Docker in platform engineering:

- Use multi-stage builds to create smaller, more secure images.
- Implement proper tagging strategies for version control and easy rollbacks.
- Utilize Docker Compose for defining and running multi-container applications.
- Regularly scan Docker images for vulnerabilities and apply security best practices.

### 3. Terraform: Infrastructure as Code Made Easy

<a href="https://www.terraform.io/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Terraform</a> is an open-source Infrastructure as Code (IaC) tool that enables you to define and provision infrastructure resources across multiple cloud providers and services.

Key features of Terraform include:

- Declarative language: Define infrastructure using a high-level configuration syntax.
- Resource graph: Visualize dependencies between resources for better planning and management.
- State management: Track the current state of your infrastructure and plan changes accordingly.
- Provider ecosystem: Support for a wide range of cloud providers and services.

Best practices for using Terraform in platform engineering:

- Use modules to create reusable and shareable infrastructure components.
- Implement remote state storage for collaboration and versioning.
- Use workspaces to manage multiple environments (e.g., development, staging, production).
- Integrate Terraform with your CI/CD pipeline for automated infrastructure updates.

### 4. GitLab CI: Continuous Integration for Seamless Development

<a href="https://docs.gitlab.com/ee/ci/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">GitLab CI</a> is a powerful continuous integration tool that integrates seamlessly with GitLab's version control system. It allows you to automate building, testing, and deploying your applications.

Key features of GitLab CI include:

- Pipeline as code: Define your CI/CD pipelines using YAML files stored in your repository.
- Auto DevOps: Automatically detect, build, test, deploy, and monitor applications.
- Container registry: Built-in Docker container registry for easy image management.
- Kubernetes integration: Native support for deploying to Kubernetes clusters.

Best practices for setting up GitLab CI workflows:

- Use GitLab CI/CD variables to store sensitive information securely.
- Implement caching to speed up subsequent pipeline runs.
- Utilize GitLab Runners to distribute workloads across multiple machines.
- Implement review apps for easier testing and collaboration on merge requests.

### 5. ArgoCD: Streamlining Continuous Deployment

<a href="https://argo-cd.readthedocs.io/en/stable/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">ArgoCD</a> is a declarative, GitOps continuous delivery tool for Kubernetes. It automates the deployment of applications to Kubernetes clusters by monitoring changes in Git repositories.

Key features of ArgoCD include:

- Automated sync: Automatically apply changes from Git to your Kubernetes clusters.
- Multi-cluster management: Manage applications across multiple Kubernetes clusters.
- Rollback and history: Easily rollback to previous versions and view deployment history.
- SSO Integration: Support for OIDC, OAuth2, LDAP, and SAML 2.0 authentication.

How to get the most out of it?

- Use the App of Apps pattern for managing multiple applications.
- Implement role-based access control (RBAC) for fine-grained permissions.
- Utilize ArgoCD's health checks to ensure application stability.
- Integrate ArgoCD with other CI tools for a complete CI/CD pipeline.

### 6. Crossplane: Unified Cloud Resource Management

<a href="https://www.crossplane.io/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Crossplane</a> extends Kubernetes with the ability to provision and manage cloud infrastructure resources using Kubernetes-style declarative configurations.

Key features of Crossplane include:

- Multi-cloud support: Manage resources across different cloud providers using a single tool.
- Custom resources: Define and use custom resources to represent cloud infrastructure.
- Composition: Create higher-level abstractions of infrastructure components.
- Kubernetes-native: Leverage existing Kubernetes tools and workflows for infrastructure management.

How to get the most out of it?

- Use Crossplane Compositions to create reusable infrastructure templates.
- Implement strong RBAC policies to control access to cloud resources.
- Utilize Crossplane's package manager to share and version infrastructure configurations.
- Integrate Crossplane with GitOps workflows for automated infrastructure updates.

### 7. Backstage: Building Developer Portals with Ease

<a href="https://backstage.io/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Backstage</a> is an open platform for building developer portals. It provides a centralized place for managing services, tools, and documentation.

Key features of Backstage include:

- Software catalogs: Maintain an inventory of all software in your organization.
- Software templates: Standardize the creation of new projects and services.
- TechDocs: Create and maintain documentation alongside your code.
- Plugin ecosystem: Extend functionality with a wide range of plugins.

How to get the most out of it?

- Use Backstage's software templates to enforce best practices and standards.
- Integrate Backstage with your existing tools and services for a unified developer experience.
- Encourage teams to maintain up-to-date documentation using TechDocs.
- Leverage Backstage's plugin ecosystem to customize the portal to your organization's needs.

### 8. Prometheus: Powerful Monitoring for Platform Engineers

<a href="https://prometheus.io/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Prometheus</a> is an open-source monitoring and alerting toolkit designed for reliability and scalability. It's particularly well-suited for monitoring dynamic container environments.

Key features of Prometheus include:

- Multi-dimensional data model: Store time series data with key-value pairs.
- PromQL: A flexible query language for analyzing collected metrics.
- Pull-based architecture: Collect metrics by scraping HTTP endpoints.
- Service discovery: Automatically discover and monitor new services.

How to get the most out of it?

- Use service discovery to automatically monitor new services and instances.
- Implement alerting rules to proactively identify and respond to issues.
- Use recording rules to pre-compute frequently used or complex queries.
- Implement proper retention and storage policies to manage data growth.

### 9. Grafana: Visualizing Platform Metrics and Logs

<a href="https://grafana.com/" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Grafana</a> is a multi-platform open-source analytics and interactive visualization web application. It provides charts, graphs, and alerts for the web when connected to supported data sources.

Key features of Grafana include:

- Data source integration: Connect to various data sources, including Prometheus, Elasticsearch, and more.
- Dashboard templates: Create and share dashboards for common use cases.
- Alerting: Set up alerts based on metric thresholds and notify through various channels.
- Panel plugins: Extend visualization capabilities with custom panel types.

How to get the most out of it?

- Create standardized dashboards for common services and applications.
- Use variables to create dynamic, reusable dashboards.
- Implement proper access controls to ensure data security.
- Utilize Grafana's alerting features to complement your existing monitoring stack.

[SigNoz](https://signoz.io/) is an open-source Application Performance Monitoring (APM) and observability tool. It provides end-to-end tracing, metrics, and logs for applications, making it an excellent choice for platform engineers focused on performance and reliability.

### Lesser-Known Yet Powerful Platform Engineering Tools

### 1. KusionStack

It is a technology stack for building cloud-native IDPs.  It helps the developers create applications with an app-centric approach. In this, Developers only need to write a single application specification AppConfiguration. AppConfiguration defines the workload and all resource dependencies without needing to supply environment-specific values and Kusion ensures it provides everything needed for the application to run.

### 2. Capsule

Capsule is an open-source framework designed for Platform Engineers to create secure, multi-tenant Internal Developer Platforms on any Kubernetes infrastructure. It supports the sharing of a single Kubernetes cluster among multiple teams or departments, improving efficiency and preventing cluster sprawl by grouping namespaces into a construct called a Tenant. 

Capsule integrates seamlessly with the  Cloud Native ecosystem which helps in enhancing the Platform Engineers' ability to build tailored and secure environments. It aims to simplify operational management and enhance resource utilization across organizations.

### 3. Spacelift

Spacelift is a CI/CD platform for Infrastructure as Code (IaC). It supports tools like Terraform, Pulumi, and AWS CloudFormation. It also integrates role-based security, custom workflows, and drift detection to manage between the desired and actual infrastructure states. It is designed for scalability. This platform offers visualization tools and automated workflows. 

### 4. Cloudsmith

Cloudsmith is a cloud-native artifact management platform that offers a fully managed solution for securely storing, managing, and distributing software packages across various formats. It enhances security with a zero-trust approach. It also includes a Dependency Firewall to scan and cache dependencies, ensuring software supply chain integrity. Cloudsmith's globally distributed infrastructure boosts productivity by reducing maintenance overhead and providing fast, reliable access to essential packages, making it a valuable tool for DevOps and development teams.

### 5. Octopus Deploy

Octopus Deploy is a robust continuous delivery (CD) platform that specializes in release orchestration and deployment automation for software teams. Founded by Paul and Sonia Stovell in 2012, it streamlines complex deployments across multi-cloud, hybrid, and on-premises environments. With features like advanced role-based access control, version control of deployment processes, and over 450 deployment steps, Octopus Deploy supports a wide range of infrastructure options and simplifies the deployment lifecycle for organizations of any scale.

## Challenges and Considerations in Platform Engineering Tool Adoption

When implementing platform engineering tools, be aware of these common pitfalls:

- Tool sprawl: Avoid adopting too many tools that serve similar purposes because if it happens, it can increase operational overhead and create difficulties in managing dependencies and troubleshooting when issues arise.
- Steep learning curves: Plan for adequate training and onboarding time for new tools because if not managed, it can reduce productivity among team members.
- Integration complexity: Ensure tools can be effectively integrated into your existing workflows. because if integration fails, it can result in workflow disruptions and production will fail which might result in business loss.
- Maintenance overhead: Consider the long-term maintenance requirements of each tool because neglecting this can lead to technical debt and increased resource allocation to tool upkeep.

To address security and compliance concerns:

- Implement strong access controls and authentication mechanisms.
- Regularly audit and update your tool stack to address vulnerabilities.
- Ensure your tools support compliance requirements relevant to your industry.

When evaluating new tools:

1. Conduct thorough proof-of-concept tests.
2. Gather feedback from potential users within your organization.
3. Assess the tool's community support and long-term viability.
4. Consider the total cost of ownership, including licensing, training, and maintenance.

## FAQs

### What is the difference between DevOps and platform engineering?

DevOps is a set of practices that combines software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops) to shorten the development lifecycle. Platform engineering, on the other hand, focuses on building and maintaining the tools and infrastructure that support DevOps practices. While DevOps is about culture and methodology, platform engineering provides the technical foundation for implementing DevOps principles effectively.

### How do I choose the right platform engineering tools for my organization?

To choose the right tools:

1. Assess your current needs and pain points.
2. Consider your team's skills and learning curve.
3. Evaluate tool integration capabilities with your existing stack.
4. Look for tools with strong community support and regular updates.
5. Consider scalability and future growth requirements.
6. Conduct proof-of-concept tests before full adoption.

### Can platform engineering tools improve application security?

Yes, platform engineering tools can significantly improve application security by:

- Enforcing consistent security policies across environments.
- Automating security scans and compliance checks.
- Providing centralized management of secrets and access controls.
- Enabling rapid patching and updates across your infrastructure.

### How do platform engineering tools impact the software development lifecycle?

Platform engineering tools impact the software development lifecycle by:

- Accelerating development through automation and self-service capabilities.
- Improving collaboration between development and operations teams.
- Enabling faster and more reliable deployments.
- Providing better visibility into application performance and issues.
- Facilitating continuous improvement through feedback loops and metrics.
